# Substitution into Formulas

- **Formulas** are equations that show how **variables** relate to each
  other.

$${Area = length \times breadth
}{A = lb}$$

- The **subject** is the **isolated variable** on the left side.

- We use formulas to find the subject when we know the other values.

1.  Write the formula.

2.  Substitute given values.

3.  Solve to find the unknown.

# Stopping Distance

- The **stopping distance** measures how far a vehicle travels between
  when the driver spots a hazard until the vehicle comes to a complete
  stop, measured in metres.

![A picture containing car, vehicle, wheel, text Description
automatically
generated](media/Formulas and Equations/media/image11.png){width="5.709597550306212in"
height="1.161849300087489in"}

$$Stopping\ distance = Reaction\ Distance + Braking\ Distance$$

**Reaction distance** is how far a vehicle travels while the driver
processes the hazard and moves their foot to the brake pedal.

**Braking distance** is how far a vehicle travels after the brakes are
applied until it stops completely. This varies based on road and vehicle
conditions.

# Blood Alcohol Content

- Blood alcohol content, or BAC, is an estimate of how much alcohol is
  in your blood.

- A BAC of 0.05 means 0.05 g of alcohol per 100 mL of your blood.

- BAC is influenced by many factors, including the number of "standard
  drinks" you consume, the amount of time in which the drinking
  occurred, a person's mass and gender.

## Time for BAC to Decrease to Zero

- Learner and Provisional drivers must have a BAC of zero.

- This is because many car collisions occur under the influence of
  alcohol.

- We can estimate the time in hours required for BAC to decrease to zero
  using the formula:

$$t = \frac{BAC}{0.015}$$

- 0.015 is the rate per hour that the liver breaks down alcohol in the
  blood (0.015g/100mL/hour).

- The **ONLY** thing that reduces BAC is the passing of time. Drinking
  coffee, taking a shower, and vomiting won't reduce BAC.

# Rearranging Formulas

## One-Step Basic Operations

- Rearranging a formula means to change the subject to another
  pronumeral.

- Isolate the pronumeral by applying inverse operations to both sides.

- Remember that the subject is always on the LHS.
